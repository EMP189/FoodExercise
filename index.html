Charred Avocado Tartare with Escamoles (Ant Larvae) and Herb Chips

Chef Jorge Vallejo, a co-founder of the acclaimed Mexico City restaurant Quintonil, created his popular charred avocado tartare with ant larvae to showcase what he believes to be “the most iconic ingredients in Mexico” — avocados and insects. The goal was also to uplift and reimagine his country’s familiar guacamole and chips; to do so, he sources Quintonil’s escamoles, which are currently in season, from a community of female harvesters in San Miguel de Allende.

Serves 6 to 8 people:

Sherry Vinaigrette

  • 3 ½ tablespoons sherry vinegar
  • 3 ½ tablespoons olive oil
  • Pinch of salt

Dehydrated Sorrel Leaf

  • 3 ½ tablespoons sorrel (leaves only)
  • 3 ½ tablespoons banana vinegar or sherry vinegar

Brown Butter With Chile and Garlic Butter

  • 9 tablespoons butter
  • 2 tablespoons garlic, finely diced
  • 2 tablespoons serrano chile (without veins or seeds), finely diced

Fried Kale

  • 1 handful kale
  • Oil

Onion and Spinach Powders

  • 1 white onion
  • 1 handful spinach

For the plating

  • 9.8 ounces escamoles
  • 2 slices avocado
  • 2 ½ teaspoons sherry vinaigrette
  • 2 serrano chiles, finely diced
  • 2 ½ teaspoons onion, finely diced
  • 5 teaspoons brown butter with chile and garlic
  • 2 ½ teaspoons dried kale
  • 2 ½ teaspoons dehydrated sorrel
  • 3 ½ tablespoons onion powder
  • 3 ½ spinach powder
  • 1 serrano chile, sliced
  • Lime zest
  • 1 tablespoon salt

Beondegi Japchae (Silkworm Pupae Cellophane Noodles)

In a nod to his Korean roots, chef Joseph Yoon created an insect-studded riff on japchae, a classic cellophane noodle dish. Yoon is the executive director of Brooklyn Bugs, a New York-based company focused on entomophagy (eating insects), which launched an eponymous culinary festival in 2017.

Serves 6 to 8 people:

  • 1 pound japchae (Korean cellophane noodles)
  • 2 cups dried silkworm pupae
  • ¾ cup soy sauce
  • 6 tablespoons mirin
  • 2 tablespoons chili oil (optional)
  • 5 cloves garlic
  • 1 teaspoon ginger
  • Salt and pepper, to taste
  • 1 onion, finely sliced lengthwise and cut in half
  • 1 red bell pepper, finely sliced lengthwise and cut in half
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 4 teaspoons sesame oil
  • Two carrots, julienned
  • 1 handful spinach
  • 6 to 10 ounces shiitake mushrooms, cut in slices

Inago no Tsukudani (Locusts Simmered in Soy Sauce and Mirin)

Chef Yoshizawa doesn’t serve any insects at Wokuni, the Japanese seafood chain from the powerhouse restaurant operator Tokyo Ichiban Foods, which also has a location in New York. But one of his formative cooking experiences is bug-centric: learning how to make inago no tsukudani, or locusts in tsukudani, a mixture of soy sauce and mirin.

Serves 4 people:

  • 1 pound locusts
  • 7 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 2 cups sake
  • 2 cups sugar
  • 1 cup mirin
